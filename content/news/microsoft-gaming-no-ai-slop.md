In a memo that circulated this week, Asha Sharma — the new CEO of Microsoft Gaming after Phil Spencer's departure — said something that deserves to be heard across the entire AI industry:

> *"We will not chase short-term efficiency or flood our ecosystem with soulless AI slop. Games are and always will be art, crafted by humans, and created with the most innovative technology provided by us."*

This is remarkable for two reasons. First, it's honest. Most tech executives speak in the careful passive voice of shareholder communications. "Soulless AI slop" is a specific diagnosis, not a hedge.

Second, it draws a distinction that most of the industry is still trying to blur: **AI as a creative tool vs. AI as a replacement for creative judgement**.

The slop problem is real and it's getting worse. Since the barrier to generating plausible-looking content dropped to near zero, every incentive system that rewards volume over quality is now flooding outputs with the cheap version. Stock image libraries, news aggregators, marketing copy, mobile game assets — the marginal cost of content is collapsing, and the signal-to-noise ratio is collapsing with it.

What Sharma is actually saying — even if she doesn't frame it this way — is that AI is only valuable when it amplifies human creative direction, not when it substitutes for having any direction at all. The difference between "AI-assisted" and "AI-generated" is human intent, human taste, and human responsibility for the output.

This principle extends far beyond games. It's the operating logic behind any AI tool that's worth using long-term: **the assistant should make the human more capable, not make the human optional**.

For the open-source AI community, this framing matters. The reason to invest in local models, controllable inference, and transparent pipelines isn't just privacy or cost — it's creative sovereignty. When you control the model, you control the direction. The output reflects your intent, not a training distribution shaped by someone else's engagement metrics.

Microsoft Gaming probably won't fully live up to this memo. Corporate incentives are hard to resist. But naming the failure mode clearly is still useful — it gives everyone a vocabulary to call it out when it happens.

No AI slop. It's a low bar. But right now, it's the right bar.
